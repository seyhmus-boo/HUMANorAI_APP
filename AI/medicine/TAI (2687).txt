Climate change significantly impacts public health, posing a multifaceted threat extending beyond traditional epidemiological frameworks. Rising temperatures exacerbate heat-related illnesses and mortality, particularly among vulnerable populations.  Changes in precipitation patterns increase the risk of waterborne diseases and vector-borne infections like malaria and dengue fever, driven by shifts in pathogen distribution and vector breeding habitats.  Extreme weather events, intensified by climate change, cause direct injuries, displacement, and mental health consequences related to trauma and loss.  Furthermore, alterations to air quality, including increased ground-level ozone and particulate matter, contribute to respiratory and cardiovascular diseases.

Practical applications focusing on mitigation and adaptation are crucial.  Mitigation strategies, such as transitioning to renewable energy and reducing greenhouse gas emissions, address the root cause.  Adaptation efforts, however, are equally vital.  These include developing early warning systems for heat waves and infectious disease outbreaks, improving water and sanitation infrastructure in vulnerable regions, and implementing public health campaigns promoting heat safety and vector control.
