Vaccination constitutes a cornerstone of modern public health, profoundly impacting the prevention and control of infectious diseases.  Its efficacy stems from the induction of a specific immune response, whereby the body develops immunological memory against a particular pathogen.  This is achieved through the administration of weakened or inactive forms of the pathogen, or specific antigens derived from it, stimulating the production of antibodies and memory B and T cells.  Consequently, upon subsequent exposure to the actual pathogen, the immune system can mount a rapid and effective response, preventing or mitigating disease manifestation.  Practical applications of vaccination are extensive, ranging from routine childhood immunizations against diseases such as measles, mumps, and rubella, effectively eradicating or significantly reducing their incidence, to targeted vaccination campaigns during outbreaks of influenza or other emerging infectious diseases.  Furthermore, vaccination programs have been instrumental in eradicating smallpox globally and are striving towards the elimination of polio.
