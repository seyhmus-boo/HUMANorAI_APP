Quantum entanglement, a cornerstone of quantum mechanics, describes a phenomenon where two or more particles become linked in such a way that their fates are intertwined, regardless of the spatial separation between them. This interconnectedness transcends classical physics, where objects possess independent properties. This is analogous to entangled particles: measuring the property of one instantly determines the corresponding property of the other, even across vast distances.  This "spooky action at a distance," as Einstein termed it, doesn't involve faster-than-light signaling, as the outcome of each individual measurement remains probabilistic until observed.  Instead, the entangled particles exist in a superposition state, a combined probability wave function, collapsing only upon measurement.  This non-classical correlation has profound implications, impacting fields like quantum computing, where entangled qubits allow for parallel computation, and quantum cryptography, leveraging the inherent unpredictability of individual measurements to ensure secure communication.  The precise nature of entanglement and its implications continue to be a subject of ongoing research and debate.
