Today's lecture focuses on the remarkable properties of carbon nanotubes (CNTs), allotropes of carbon with exceptional characteristics stemming from their unique cylindrical nanostructure.  CNTs exhibit extraordinary tensile strength, surpassing that of steel by a significant margin, coupled with remarkable flexibility. Their high aspect ratio contributes to exceptional electrical conductivity, comparable to copper,  while specific types demonstrate semiconducting behaviour, opening possibilities for diverse applications.  Furthermore, their thermal conductivity is superior to most materials, making them ideal for heat dissipation.

These exceptional properties translate to a broad range of practical applications.  In materials science, CNTs enhance the strength and conductivity of composites, finding use in lightweight yet robust aerospace components and high-performance sporting goods.  Their electrical characteristics are exploited in advanced electronics, such as field-effect transistors and transparent conductive films for flexible displays.  Moreover, their high surface area contributes to their utility in energy storage devices like batteries and supercapacitors, as well as in catalysis.  Ongoing research continues to explore the full potential of these versatile nanomaterials.
