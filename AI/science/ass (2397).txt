DNA replication is the semiconservative process by which a cell creates an identical copy of its DNA molecule.  Imagine DNA as a zipper, with each side representing a strand composed of nucleotide bases.  During replication, this zipper is carefully unzipped by enzymes, separating the two strands.  Each separated strand then serves as a template for the synthesis of a new complementary strand.  This is analogous to using a stencil:  the original strand dictates the precise sequence of nucleotides added to the new strand, ensuring fidelity.  Enzymes like DNA polymerase act as skilled construction workers, adding nucleotides to the growing strand in a 5' to 3' direction.  Leading and lagging strands are formed due to the unidirectional nature of polymerase, resulting in the creation of Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand which are subsequently joined.
