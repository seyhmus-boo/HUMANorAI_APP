Good morning. Today's lecture focuses on CRISPR-Cas9, a revolutionary gene-editing technology transforming genetic engineering.  CRISPR, derived from a bacterial defense mechanism, utilizes a guide RNA molecule to direct the Cas9 enzyme to a specific DNA sequence.  This targeted complex then creates a double-stranded break, allowing for precise gene modification through mechanisms like non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), resulting in gene knockouts, or homology-directed repair (HDR), enabling gene insertion or correction.

The implications of this technology are profound. In medicine, CRISPR holds immense promise for treating genetic diseases like cystic fibrosis and sickle cell anemia through targeted gene correction in somatic cells.  Further research explores its potential in eradicating viral infections, such as HIV, by excising viral DNA.  Agricultural applications include enhancing crop yields by modifying genes for pest resistance and improving nutritional content.  However, ethical considerations, including off-target effects and germline editing, necessitate careful regulation and responsible research practices.
